### **1. Jisho.org**

Probably the easiest starting point. Search a word like **学生**, and it gives the vocab meaning, reading, and individual kanji links. You can click **学** and **生** separately to see each kanji’s meanings/readings. Jisho is built around interlinked word, kanji, and sentence lookups. ([Free Language][1])

Example workflow:
Search **学生** → see “student” → click **学** → see “study, learning” → click **生** → see “life, genuine, birth, student,” etc.

### **2. Kanshudo**

Very good for learners because it combines dictionary, kanji, components, example sentences, and study tools. Its quick search covers kanji, words, example sentences, grammar points, and names, and it includes kanji/component information. ([Kanshudo][2])

This may be the closest to the “breakdown” style you want.

### **3. Kanji Alive**

Great for individual kanji. It shows meaning, readings, radicals/components, stroke order, and examples. It is more kanji-focused than vocab-focused, but excellent for understanding what each character contributes. ([Kanji alive][3])

### **4. Takoboto**

A clean Japanese-English dictionary with word search, example sentences, and kanji information for each word. It also has apps, which is handy if you study on your phone. ([Takoboto][4])

### **5. Kanjicards.org**

Free printable PDF kanji cards. These include readings, meanings, and example words with English translations, so it is useful if you want a book/flashcard-style resource. ([Kanjicards][5])

My recommendation: use **Jisho** for quick lookup, **Kanshudo** when you want deeper breakdowns, and **Kanji Alive** when you want to understand the individual kanji.
